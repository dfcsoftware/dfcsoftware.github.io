Walking down the street a bank robbery occurs and a guy walking by, Sam, is shot in the leg. Falling to the ground, by-standers gasp “How horrible is that!”

So, like the farmer who lost his horse[1] “It could be good, could be bad, who knows?”

Is it is, or is it ain’t

  1. Is Sam a good guy?
  2. What is the impact of this event on Sam’s life?
  3. What if it makes no difference? Sam just tripped over a dog, our facts were wrong at the time.

In order to understand the world I experience around me I usually classify things. Red, green, grey, and so on, based on my own life. Taking other people’s perceived experience, and my own desire to be right, I form decisions and normally express them.

It seems that these things affect our outcome

  1. Life experience - everyone is different
  2. Social norms at the time - peer discussions
  3. Crowd thinking - a few people, influence a lot quickly

For example, if most of your exposure about Green Martians is bad, you will tend to classify GM as bad. Alternatively if you have a good friend you trust who likes GM you may decide GM are good. And of course you may have little interest or desire to think about GM and classify them an “they don’t bother me, maybe ok.”

Posts flock, right, ratio, and cult discussed some reasons why I think the way I do.

Thought Control

As an experiment, I take someone I like, and imagine hating them, or someone I hate and imagine liking them. It opens up lots of possibilities.

I have found that nothing is ever black and white, it is shades of grey, but I forget that because my world is a big place I am busy, mostly. The slippery slope of change exists everywhere and time does change things.

But I always come back to “A person should decide something and hold onto that, unless reliable new information is available.” My best.

Regards,

– Don Cohoon
